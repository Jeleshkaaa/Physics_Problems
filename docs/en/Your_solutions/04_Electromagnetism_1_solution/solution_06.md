# Physics Problem: Electric Field from a System of Charges

This repository contains a step-by-step derivation and calculation for the electric field intensity generated by two point charges located on the x-axis.

---

## 1. System Configuration
* **Charge 1 ($q_1$):** $+q$ at position $\vec{r}_1 = (-a, 0)$
* **Charge 2 ($q_2$):** $+2q$ at position $\vec{r}_2 = (a, 0)$
* **Total Charge:** $3q$

---

## 2. General Field Vectors

### The General Case: $\vec{E}(x, y)$
Using the superposition principle, the field at any point $P(x, y)$ is the vector sum of fields from both charges.
$$\vec{E}(x, y) = kq \left[ \frac{(x + a)\hat{i} + y\hat{j}}{((x + a)^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} + \frac{2((x - a)\hat{i} + y\hat{j})}{((x - a)^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} \right]$$

### Specific Case: $\vec{E}(0, y)$ (On the y-axis)
When $x=0$, the distances to both charges are equal ($r = \sqrt{a^2 + y^2}$).
$$\vec{E}(0, y) = \frac{kq}{(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2}} (-a\hat{i} + 3y\hat{j})$$

### Specific Case: $\vec{E}(x, 0)$ (On the x-axis)
When $y=0$, the field is strictly horizontal.
$$\vec{E}(x, 0) = kq \left[ \frac{(x + a)}{|x + a|^3}\hat{i} + \frac{2(x - a)}{|x - a|^3}\hat{i} \right]$$

---

## 3. Equilibrium: Finding the Zero Field Point
To find where $\vec{E} = 0$, we analyze the components:
1.  **Vertical ($E_y$):** Must be zero, which only occurs on the x-axis ($y = 0$).
2.  **Horizontal ($E_x$):** Between the charges, the fields oppose each other. Setting them equal:
    $$\frac{kq}{(x+a)^2} = \frac{k(2q)}{(a-x)^2} \implies \frac{1}{x+a} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{a-x}$$
    Solving for $x$:
    $$x = a \frac{1 - \sqrt{2}}{1 + \sqrt{2}} \approx \mathbf{-0.17a}$$

---

## 4. Numerical Calculation
**Given:** $a = 0.2 \text{ m}$, $y = 0.3 \text{ m}$, $q = 2 \, \mu\text{C}$

**Step 1: Distance Denominator**
$$(a^2 + y^2)^{3/2} = (0.2^2 + 0.3^2)^{1.5} \approx 0.04687$$

**Step 2: Vector Calculation**
$$\vec{E}(0, 0.3) \approx \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(2 \times 10^{-6})}{0.04687} (-0.2\hat{i} + 0.9\hat{j})$$
$$\vec{E} \approx \mathbf{(-7.66 \times 10^4 \hat{i} + 3.45 \times 10^5 \hat{j}) \text{ V/m}}$$

---

## 5. Limit Investigation ($y \gg a$)
When the observation point is very far ($y \gg a$), the two charges appear as a single point source.
* **Point Charge Approximation:** The total charge is $Q = q + 2q = 3q$.
* **Mathematical Limit:** As $y \to \infty$, the $x$-component vanishes and the $y$-component reduces to:
  $$\vec{E} \approx \frac{k(3q)}{y^2} \hat{j}$$
This confirms that at great distances, the geometry of the charges becomes irrelevant, and the field follows the standard inverse-square law for a charge of $3q$.
